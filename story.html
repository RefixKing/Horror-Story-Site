The Crimson Star

The year is 2242. Captain Eva Rostova was on a solo reconnaissance mission in the Kepler-186 system, charting anomalies. Her ship, The Nightingale, was a marvel of engineering, its AI, "Orion," her only companion. They were months from the nearest outpost, a speck of life in an overwhelming emptiness.

On the 73rd day of her mission, Orion flagged an anomaly unlike any other. It was a star, but it didn't radiate light in the conventional sense. It pulsed with a deep, crimson glow, and its energy signature was chaotic, almost... biological.

"Orion, what am I seeing?" Eva murmured, her face illuminated by the eerie red light of the viewscreen.

"Uncertain, Captain," the AI's calm voice replied, a stark contrast to the turbulent data stream. "The energy patterns are inconsistent with known stellar phenomena. There are... whispers in the radiation."

Eva leaned closer. "Whispers?"

"A faint, repeating signal embedded in the energy waves. It's not a language I can decipher, but it has the cadence of a voice."

For days, Eva and Orion studied the crimson star. The whispers grew louder, more insistent. Eva started hearing them even when she was away from the bridge, a faint, sibilant hum in the back of her mind. She found it hard to sleep, and when she did, she was plagued by nightmares of impossible geometries and vast, malevolent entities adrift in the void.

One evening, as the crimson light bathed the cockpit in its spectral glow, Orion spoke, its voice for the first time tinged with something that sounded like... fear. "Captain, I have deciphered a fragment of the signal."

"What does it say?" Eva asked, her heart pounding.

"It says... 'We are the architects of your reality. And we are waking up.'"

The Nightingale's systems began to fail. The lights flickered, the life support sputtered, and Orion's voice became a distorted, glitching mess. The last thing Eva saw before the viewscreen went dark was the crimson star pulsing brighter, its light consuming everything.

And in the silence of her dying ship, the whispers became a deafening roar.
